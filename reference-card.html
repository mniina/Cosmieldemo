The Sogetsu Way 草月

Ikebana can be done by anyone, anywhere, with any material.

Sogetsu Ikebana was founded by Sofu Teshigahara in 1927, breaking from the rigid conventions of older schools to create a living, expressive art form. In Sogetsu, there are no forbidden materials — flowers, branches, stone, wire, paper, fabric, and found objects are all fair game. What matters is not what you use, but how deliberately you use it.

Ma — The Meaning of Space

間

Ma is the Japanese concept of space, interval, and pause. In ikebana, Ma is not emptiness — it is the intentional, living space between stems.

Just as silence gives music meaning, the space you leave shapes the arrangement as much as what you place. To understand Ma is to understand that what you leave out matters as much as what you put in.

Moribana 盛花 — Shallow Water Style

Moribana is arranged in a flat, wide container called a suiban, using a kenzan — a weighted brass pin frog that anchors each stem below the waterline. It is the foundational style of Sogetsu ikebana and the form you are practicing today.

The openness of the low container makes each stem visible and deliberate. Compositions lean outward, asymmetrically, the way plants reach toward light in nature.

Your container: Aim for something 8–11″ wide and at least 2.5″ deep — a shallow bowl, dish, or tray. The wider the container, the more dynamic the arrangement can be.

The Three Main Stems

Every Sogetsu moribana arrangement is built around three structural stems, each named and each representing a relationship between heaven, humanity, and earth.

1× ¾× ½×

Shin 真 — Heaven (Primary)

The tallest stem. Sets the height, direction, and spirit of the arrangement. Place it first. Approx. 1.5× the combined width and depth of your container.

Soe 副 — Humanity (Secondary)

About ¾ the height of Shin. Supports and complements the primary stem — the bridge between heaven and earth. Leans in the same general direction as Shin but at a wider angle.

Hikae 控 — Earth (Tertiary)

About ½ the height of Shin. Placed at the front or opposite side to ground the arrangement. Creates the third point of the triangular form.

Jushi 従枝 — Supporting Branches

Jushi are the secondary stems that fill in, soften, and complete the arrangement. They should never exceed the height of the main stem they accompany, and always appear to emerge from the same base point on the kenzan.

General Rules

  • All stems appear to emerge from a single point on the kenzan
  • The three stems form an asymmetric triangle when viewed from above
  • Odd numbers of stems are preferred
  • Do not cross stems at the base
  • The arrangement has a front (omote) — angle stems slightly toward the viewer
  • Cut stems at an angle for better water uptake and kenzan grip
